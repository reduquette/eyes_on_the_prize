Summary

The episode begins with the Chicago Freedom Movement.

Dr King travels to Chicago to stage a nonviolent campaign for civil rights in the north, where, despite the passage of the civil rights act of 1964 and the voting rights act of 1965, de facto segregation is as strong as ever. In Chicago, they are faced with the opposition of Mayor Daley, whose iron control extends beyond Chicago into the national Democratic party. Daley stymies King not by outright opposition but by announcing an initiative to correct each issue King raises, from poor housing conditions to failing public services, preventing the kind of confrontation necessary to gain national attention-- and preventing real permanent change.

Attempting to expose white resistance to civil rights, the movement shifts focus to white flight and housing segregation. Real estate agents in the Chicago area kept up segregation by only showing listings in white suburbs to white individuals, refusing to offer those listings to Black people. White people in those suburbs were likewise opposed to Black people moving into those neighborhoods.

On Sunday, July 10, 1966, after a rally at Soldier Field, Dr King marches to city hall to place a list of demands on the door. Shortly thereafter, riots break out after police shut off water hydrants, which functioned as replacements for inaccessible community pools in the hot Chicago summer. While King and other members of the Chicago Freedom movement attempt to persuade people to halt the violence, the incident worsens the movement’s already tenuous relationship with Mayor Daley. Housing marches continue throughout the summer, facing crowds worse even than those in the south.

Detroit Uprising of 1967

In Detroit, in the summer of 1967, the latest of a string of race riots across the nation breaks out. The precipitating incident is a police shutdown of a “blind pig”, an illegal after-hours club.

In the aftermath, Johnson appoints a commission to investigate the cause of the riots of that summer. Their report, which points to comprehensive denial of opportunities to Black people, is ignored by the sitting president.

Question 1

In Chicago, Jesse Jackson calls out that racism in the north is perceived as subtle, but is not subtle at all. What does he mean when he says this, and how does this relate to the goals of King’s nonviolent direct action program in Chicago? How do you see “subtle” racism at Boston College?

Chicago Freedom Movement

Counter Protesters

Besides the clarity of physical, tangible assault, racism manifested itself in ways that were anything but subtle.

While segregation in the south was codified into law, segregation in the north was created and perpetuated by a system of discrimination and inequal opportunity. In so doing, it retained the cover of plausible deniability, that segregation was “just how things turned out”. White liberals in the north could decry the racism of the south while ignoring the racism of their own cities— and in themselves. Racism hid behind concerns over safety: white people have to move out of neighborhoods when Black people move in, one person claims in the chapter, because it isn’t safe to walk the streets at night. At the same time, the quality of city services to an area was dictated by its racial makeup.

Chicago Freedom Movement

King at Soldier Field

A significant part of the goal of the Chicago Freedom Movement was to try to combat the clear and unequal division in the north by stripping away the gentle inattention that enforced northern segregation. In a speech in 1966, Dr. King called for “a confrontation in the city of Chicago between the forces resisting change and the forces demanding change”(Chicago Freedom). Daley knew this, and prevented real progress by addressing small concerns King raised, such as a lack of garbage upkeep in a neighborhood, or unsafe conditions in housing ("King Pounds"). King and movement leaders found a way to accomplish their goal by taking protests and marches into white suburbs of Chicago, where they were met with clear resistance and violence.

Boston College Protest

Students protesting Black Lives Don't Matter signs

The Black@BostonCollege instagram account, which gained attention summer 2020, details some of the many ways subtle or covert racism flourishes at Boston College. It is “subtle” when Boston College Police Department officers stop Black students on campus ("When I was a sophomore..."). It is “subtle” when only the section of a dorm housing majority Black students is vandalized, and the vandals claim they didn’t know ("Regarding recent hate crimes"). It is “subtle” when BC’s student body is 4% Black, while the US population is over 13% Black, and university administrators point to a lack of qualified applicants instead of their own failure to search out students or cultivate a welcoming environment (Sarci).

Question 2

In Letter from a Birmingham jail, Martin Luther King decries the passivity and restraint of the “white moderate”. How do you see those characteristics present themselves in white leaders who impede the push for civil rights in the north?

Next question

In Letter From a Birmingham Jail, Dr. King writes,

the Negro's great stumbling block in his stride toward freedom is not the White Citizen's Councilor or the Ku Klux Klanner, but the white moderate, who is more devoted to “order” than to justice; who prefers a negative peace which is the absence of tension to a positive peace which is the presence of justice; who constantly says, “I agree with you in the goal you seek, but I cannot agree with your methods of direct action”; who paternalistically believes he can set the timetable for another mans freedom; who lives by a mythical concept of time and who constantly advises the Negro the wait for a “more convenient season.”

In Chicago, Mayor Daley does his best to impede the movement while appearing in support of the movement’s goals. When Dr. King moves into a slum to bring light to the conditions faced by Black residents of Chicago, King’s apartment is swiftly renovated and the building’s heating restored ("Photo Essay"). After meeting with Dr King and movement leaders, Daley says that they agree on the goals, the question is just how to achieve them— a question that, according to him, has been given no answer. After the same summit, Dr King lists proposals of solutions that were rejected by Daley. The missing answers are not missing at all, merely ignored.

White religious leaders shared in some of Daley's animosity towards King and the Chicago Freedom movement. Archbishop Cody urged restraint on multiple occaisions, and in FBI documents, it is revealed he felt members of his diocese were perhaps "over zealous" in their support of civil rights (qtd in Herguth).

The actions of the white moderate have drastic consequences. The Kerner commission, investigating 1967 race riots in Detroit, Chicago, and Newark, among other cities, found that these riots were “foreshadowed by an accumulation of unresolved grievances and by widespread dissatisfaction” especially with the “unwillingness or inability of local government to respond.” ("National Advisory") In other words, the riots could well have been prevented if officials who in theory supported civil rights had acted out that support with the power of their positions. Quoting John F. Kennedy, Dr King himself prophesied that “those who will make this peaceful revolution impossible will make a violent revolution inevitable”.

Detroit Riots

President Johnson sending federal troops

Faced with this indictment of his own role in the riots, President Johnson ignores the commission’s recommendations. He refuses to receive their report. It was his own question— what can be done to prevent further riots— that the commission sought to answer; their answer was not amenable to Johnson. In this moment of apathy, we are reminded of Johnson’s slip recounted in the documentary, when he didn’t want to see federal troops shooting a pregnant [n-word]. One wonders, would any answer, any explanation for the violence, been acceptable to Johnson? Or did he just want to appear to care, caring not to take action?

Question 3

Who and what is deemed violent in this chapter? What elements of violence are overlooked?

Next question

Detroit Riots

Fire

Even physical violence was not lacking in the North. After being hit in the back of the neck at a march in Marquette Park, Dr. King said “I have seen many demonstrations in the south but I have never seen anything so hostile and so hateful as I have seen here today.” ("Photo Esssay")

The clearest and most obvious answer to this question is the violence of the riots. The Detroit riot is examined in detail in this chapter, and there are numerous testimonials from individuals shocked at the violence. Accounts from newspapers describe the violence and destruction of property in detail, and the horror of those living there. Fire and gunfire made the streets of Detroit a warzone.

Racism in the north is often regarded as less violent than in the south, and in some ways it was— it was, perhaps, less overtly violent. Yet discrimination, segregation, and racism need not be physically combative to be violent: “Yes, we are tired of being lynched physically in Mississippi,” Dr. King said, in his speech to the Chicago Freedom rally, “and we are tired of being lynched spiritually and economically in the North.” A central goal of the Chicago Freedom movement was to bring attention to the horrendous conditions of housing available to Black residents ("Dr King Tells"). What is the absence of adequate heating, plumbing, safe electricity, or shelter if not the use of physical force or power?

Question 4

Compare the roles of the police in the 1967 Detroit riot and 2020 Minneapolis George Floyd protests.

Next question

Detroit Riot

National Guardsmen

In both cases, the riots were the result of pent-up anger over continued patterns of police violence and abuse of power. Both were likewise part of a larger pattern of violence. Detroit was the climactic event in a series of riots across the United States during the summer of 1967; Minneapolis was the inciting event in a series of protests across the United States and the globe during the summer of 2020.

Minneapolis Protest

Police

The way the police interacted with Black citizens of Detroit during the riot recalls countless stories of modern police violence and brutality. Witnesses describe the police urging someone to run so that they could shoot him. Three Black teenagers were found dead in a motel, killed by two police officers during the riot. The 43rd victim of the riot was deaf, and could not hear the police telling him to stop. In the chapter, a Black city official recalls being stopped in his car by police, despite having done nothing wrong. Prisoners were beaten and kept in abysmal conditions.

Question 5

This chapter highlighted areas where civil rights leaders diverged in their guidance towards the Black community, particularly in regards to the march to Cicero in Chicago. Why do they disagree? What do you think was the right answer, and why?

Next question

Chicago Freedom Movement

Press Conference

The perception of the civil rights movement is often that the Black community, with the exception of a few individuals like Malcom X, was united under Dr. Martin Luther King’s wisdom and guidance. Community unity and organizing did play an indispensable role, but the Black community, and the community of civil rights activists, were far from wholly united behind one banner. Perspectives differed on how to effect the change they all saw as necessary— in particular, on nonviolence.

Chicago Freedom Movement

Protest at real estate office

Dr. King was a steadfast and staunch advocate for nonviolence. In his speech at Soldier Field in Chicago, he argued that nonviolence was essential to the progress that had been made: “Our movement's adherence to nonviolence has been a major factor in the creation of a moral climate that has made progress possible”. Logistical reasons as well as moral ones played a role in his strategy. “The ultimate weakness of a riot is that it can be halted by superior force,” he went on. “We have neither the techniques, the numbers nor the weapons to win a violent campaign.” ("Chicago Freedom")

For some, Dr. King was too extreme. Mayor Daley and Archbishop Cody were two such individuals, opposed to the marches King encouraged. They felt he incited violence even by his presence. They were joined in this view by Dr. Jackson, president of the National Baptist convention, who wrote that there was “danger of using nonviolence in such a way that it will create violence”. Before King’s arrival in Chicago, there had not been widespread violence or conflict, and he supported marches which lead invariably to such conflict.

For others, nonviolence could not go far enough. Community organizers of the Congress for Race Equality (CORE) carried on with the march into Cicero, even after it was called off by Chicago freedom movement leaders following successful summit talks. They felt that Dr. King was too quickly appeased or too concerned with maintaining the peace, and that a summit agreement was worth nothing if it did not effect actual change. King’s strategy might have been effective in the South, where he was met with more outright resistance, but in Chicago he was “punching a pillow”. What use was there in continuing with methods that did not work? The rank-and-file members of the Chicago freedom movement were, in their own words, “a new breed of cat”, willing to go farther than Dr. King in direct action.

Throughout the chapter, civil rights leaders espouse a theology of liberation. Many, if not most, of the individuals involved in the chicago reedom movement were religious leaders. The idea of Christ as being on the side of the oppressed, and their work as being in the legacy of Christ, appears throughout.

The Rev. Martin Luther King marches to city hall to place a list of demands on the door in a clear parallel to the actions of his namesake, Martin Luther. Moments before, he gave an address to the rally at Soldier Field in which he referenced a song about Joshua and Jericho, concluding that the battle was in their hands. Just as marching brought down the walls of Jericho, it would bring down the walls of segregation and discrimination. Referencing Ephesians, he calls for them to put on the “breastplate of righteousness” and the “full armor of God”. In so doing, he implicitly aligns opposition from authority to open housing with “the devil’s schemes” (Ephesians 6:11-12). Similarly, the Rev. Jesse Jackson compares the cost of the marches to “the continuation of a kind of sin”, calling racism a sin.

Chicago Freedom Movement

Young counter protesters

It is worth noting that religion is not uniquely the weapon of the forces for civil rights. The Catholic archbishop John Cody’s archives are full of letters, marked “race mail”, criticizing Cody’s support of the civil rights movement. Self-described “real good and sincere catholics” were opposed to integration on the grounds that God designed two distinct races and must have intended for them to remain separate (qtd. in Cressler 273-274). While this kind of argument might be verboten today, it was not astonishing less than sixty years ago.
